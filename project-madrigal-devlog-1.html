Week 1: Background Research

The main thing I accomplished this week was reading through and compiling research regarding the potential for Project Madrigal. This included reading through several technical papers on computer graphics, as well as finding data on relevant industries for Project Madrigal.

Here are three technical resources that are especially relevant to Project Madrigal.

1. Sphere Tracing: A Geometric Method for the Antialiased Ray Tracing of Implicit Surfaces

This paper by John C. Hart introduces the now classic technique of sphere-tracing. Although the paper contains "ray tracing" in the title, this technique is often used today for raymarching implicit surfaces with signed distance functions. It involves casting rays into a scene and stepping forward along the ray at varying spherical distances to check if geometry is intersected.

2. Rendering a Sphere on a Quad

This article by Ben Golus takes a deep dive into generating raytraced imposter objects that can blend into a scene next to rasterized objects. Golus uses Unity and documents the process and theory of getting a raytraced sphere (not a typical sphere mesh), to work seamlessly with Unity's shadowing and lighting systems.

3. My Journey Into Fractals

This is an article by Gregory Ivanov about the technical details and development of the game "Yedoma Globula." (YG). YG is unique because it features a fractal world (rendered through raymarching), that the player can explore. The thing that really makes YG stand out is the immense, beautiful detail of the fractals, and the fact that there are physics collisions that take place between the first person player and the raymarched fractals.

Relevant Products

The companies above give a good idea of who some of the heavy hitters are in the gaming industry, and some of them even develop technologies that could utilize the work done in Project Madrigal or compete with it. I will now go over five suchg products that either address similar problems to Project Madrigal or those that could benefit from it.

1. Marble Marcher

Marble Marcher is a game developed by a developer who goes by CodeParade. Marble Marcher is interesting in that solves some of the problems Project Madrigal aims to solve. Specifically, it is comprised of fractal levels that you must roll around in to capture a flag and complete the level. The fractals use raymarching, and there is interactivity in the game due to the physics between the player and the raymarched solids. You can also play a version of the game on ShaderToy here (the whole game was ported into a fragment shader!) Marble Marcher could actually benefit from the work done in Project Madrigal, as the aim would be to create ways in which rasterized objects could be added to flesh out the game even further.

2. Yedoma Globula

Yedoma Globula is a game developed by Greg Ivanov. The reason it is relevant to Project Madrigal is similar to why Marble Marcher was -- it features a raymarched, fractal world that the player can explore. However, the lighting and visuals of Yedoma are of extremely high quality and look even close to AAA standards. Interestingly, there looks to be some rasterized geoemtry in the game that sits on the raymarched fractals -- the interactions between them with respect to shadowing seem to be fleshed out but faked slightly. There also does not appear to be any sort of physics between the rasterized and raymarched objects. This, specifically, is where the work in Project Madrigal could be useful to this wonderful project.

3. Raymarching Toolkit for Unity

The Raymarching Toolkit for Unity is a plugin for Unity that allows users to add raymarched objects to their scene (a scene that can also contain rasterized geometry). However, the toolkit has some limitations, such as that the raymarched objects are not seamlessly integrated into Unity's lighting and shadowing, and they are not accurately collideable. As a result, the work in the toolkit currently seems to be best for background objects or imposters (the graphics kind, not the Among Us kind!). I think work done in Project Madrigal would be even be relevant to this project, as seamlessly integrating raymarching into Unity is even on the roadmap for the raymarching toolkit.

4. Unity (Game Engine)

This product is more broad than the others, but I believe the work in Project Madrigal could be useful to Unity as a whole, with potential to work as a plugin. Being able to have interactive raymarched geometry in scenes would allow gamemakers to develop more kinds of games with art styles that don't work as well in rasterized geometry. Having seamless integration of raymarched geometry in the engine would also be a nice way to help gamedevs create interesting and stylistic environments with potentially less work.

5. Unreal Engine

Unreal has actually taken some rendering steps in the past few years that would provide interesting ways the kind of work in Project Madrigal could be useful. Namely, Unreal accomplishes a lot of their shadowing and occlusion by using Signed Distance Field techniques, where these fields and functions are generated for rasterized scene geometry offline. A lot of this is similar to how shadowing would be accomplished in raymarching, which would make high quality raymarched solids integrate well with Unreal's existing technology.
